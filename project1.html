Eternal Wealth Bank

亲爱的家人们，亲爱的乡邻们， 我从天堂写下这封信，因为在这里，我每天都被你们燃烧的纸钱、金银珠宝所“包围”。那些纸扎的豪宅、汽车、佣人，伴着熊熊火焰而来，据说是为了让我在这里也能过上富足的生活。 我看见你们围在我的灵位前，摆上水果、菜肴、酒杯，点燃蜡烛、香炉，就像我们仍旧围坐一桌吃饭一样。你们每次给酒杯添酒时，我仿佛还能听见你们用力抿唇、对邻居微笑的声音，仿佛是在告诉所有人：“看，我们的Agnes死后依然享受如此丰盛，我们的孝心无处不在。” 可你们知道吗？其实在这个天堂里，我什么都不需要。那些堆积成山的纸钱不会在这片虚无中变成真正的财富，烧毁的蜡烛也不会给我的灵魂带来温暖。你们所做的一切，真的与我无关。因为这个热闹的葬礼从头到尾，从未为我而设。 在这场表演里，我不过是背景。真正的主角是你们，是那些需要证明自己孝顺的子女，是那些渴望展现家庭地位的父母，是那些想让整个村庄知道自己”成器”的子孙。我能听见那些邻居在背后议论：“他们的孩子事业真成功。”“看，这一大家人真孝顺，送了那么多东西给Agnes。”你们通过这场葬礼，重新巩固了你们在社会关系中的位置，借着我的死亡，来成就你们活着时的荣光。 我在这里看着，看着你们演奏的乐器，看着那充满仪式感的每一个动作，心里说不出的凄凉。死亡本该是我最后的安息，却变成了你们热闹的社交活动。每一次火光升起，我看见的不是对逝者的怀念，而是你们对生活的焦虑和对外界评价的在意。 生者啊，你们忙于为自己搭建心灵的避风港，却用我作为代价。可曾有人想过，我是否真正需要这些纸钱、这些繁华的送别？或许，我真正想要的，只是几分钟的静谧，几句真诚的悼念。 但我知道，这些都不会发生。因为在这个世界里，葬礼从来不属于死者。 永别了， Agnes

Dear family and friends, I write this letter from heaven, where every day I am “surrounded” by the paper money, gold, and jewelry you burn for me. The paper mansions, cars, and servants arrive in flames, supposedly to ensure I live a prosperous life here in the afterlife. I see you gathered around my altar, placing fruits, dishes, and cups of wine, lighting candles and incense as if I were still at the table with you. Each time you refill the cups, I can almost hear the sound of your lips pursing into polite smiles for the neighbors, as if saying, “Look at how well our Agnes is being taken care of, even in death. Our filial piety is unmatched.” But do you know the truth? Here in heaven, I need none of this. The piles of paper money will never become real wealth in this void, and the burning candles won’t warm my spirit. All that you do is, in fact, nothing to do with me. This grand funeral was never truly for me. I am merely the backdrop in this performance. The real protagonists are you—the children eager to prove their devotion, the parents keen to show their family’s status, and the descendants hoping the whole village knows how “successful” they’ve become. I can hear the neighbors whisper, “Their kids must be doing so well.” “Look, what a filial family, they’ve sent so much to Agnes.” Through this ceremony, you’re rebuilding your social standing, using my death to bolster your pride in life. I watch it all from here—the music, the rituals, every carefully executed gesture—and all I feel is a cold sense of detachment. Death, which should have been my final rest, has become your lively social event. Each time the flames rise, I see not a gesture of remembrance, but your fear of judgment and your obsession with external validation. You, the living, busy yourselves creating a refuge for your own hearts, but you do so at my expense. Did anyone stop to think if I truly needed all this paper wealth and this ostentatious farewell? Perhaps all I ever wanted was a moment of quiet, a few words of sincere remembrance. But I know that won’t happen. In this world, funerals were never meant for the dead. Farewell, Agnes
